How non-residency affects your TFSA

A TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account) holder who becomes a non-resident of Canada may continue to hold their TFSA. However, there are important rules and possible tax implications to consider.

What is a non-resident

You are a non-resident for income tax purposes if you meet any of the following conditions:

  • You normally, customarily, or routinely live in another country and are not considered a resident of Canada

  • You do not have residential ties in Canada and one of the following applies:

    • You live outside Canada throughout the year
    • You stay in Canada for less than 183 days in the tax year

Understanding your residency status is important in determining if you can contribute to your TFSA and if taxes may apply to contributions you make.

Even if you no longer live in Canada, you may have residential ties in Canada that are enough for you to be considered a factual or deemed resident of Canada. In these cases, the regular rules for holding or opening a TFSA still apply.

Review residential ties to Canada

Residential ties to Canada include:

  • a home in Canada
  • a spouse or common-law partner or dependants in Canada
  • personal property in Canada, such as a car or furniture
  • social ties in Canada

Other ties that may be relevant include:

  • Canadian driver's license
  • Canadian bank accounts or credit cards
  • hospitalization and medical insurance coverage from a province or territory of Canada

Tax implications for non-resident TFSA holders

You may keep your TFSA

If you become a non-resident, you are allowed to keep your existing TFSA. Any income you earn in your account, such as interest, dividends, or capital gains, will not be taxed in Canada.

However, you will not accumulate any new available contribution room for any year in which you are a non-resident for the entire year. Contribution room created as a result of a non-resident withdrawal will only become available when you regain residency. The annual dollar limit is not pro-rated in the year you emigrate or immigrate.

You may withdraw from your TFSA

As a non-resident, you may withdraw funds from your TFSA without being taxed. However, the contribution room created from a non-resident withdrawal will only become available when you establish residency again.

Example: How a non-resident withdrawal affects contribution room

You withdraw $5,000 from your TFSA when you are a non-resident. The next calendar year, $5,000 will be added back to your contribution room, but it will not be available to you until you become a resident of Canada again.

Do not contribute to your TFSA

Only residents of Canada may contribute to their TFSA tax-free. You can contribute to your TFSA up to the date that you become a non-resident. After that it will be considered a taxable non-resident contribution.

How non-resident contributions are taxed

Any non-resident contribution you make, except for a qualifying transfer or an exempt contribution, is subject to a 1% tax for each month the contribution remains in the account. This tax continues to apply until you withdraw the non-resident contribution or you become a resident of Canada again.

You will receive a notice of assessment for any non-resident contribution you make.

If the non-resident contribution also exceeds your available TFSA contribution room, you may be subject to an additional 1% monthly tax on the excess amount.

If you are a non-resident beneficiary of a TFSA

If you are a non-resident and are named as a beneficiary of a TFSA, there are possible tax implications.

There are two types of beneficiaries:

  • Successor holder
  • Designated beneficiary

Non-resident successor holder

A successor holder is the survivor of the deceased who is named as the new holder of the TFSA in the TFSA contract or will of the deceased holder.

Non-resident successor holders who do not have a social insurance number (SIN) should apply for an individual tax number (ITN).

To apply for an ITN: Application for a CRA Individual Tax Number (ITN) for Non-Residents (Form T1261).

Non-resident successor holders cannot make contributions to the TFSA while they are non-resident. However, they may withdraw funds at any time.

For more information about successor holders and possible tax implications, refer to If you are a successor holder.

Non-resident designated beneficiary

A designated beneficiary may be a survivor, family member, or other person or organization named as a beneficiary of the TFSA in the TFSA contract or will of the deceased holder.

A non-resident designated beneficiary of a TFSA trust may receive a payment in cash or in kind based on the fair market value (FMV) of the TFSA on the date of death.

However, the designated beneficiary must report payment amounts they receive during the exempt period that are above the FMV of the TFSA. Any amounts received are subject to non-resident withholding tax.

You can report these amounts to the CRA using the following: Statement of Amounts Paid or Credited to Non-Residents of Canada (Form NR4).

For more information: If you are a designated beneficiary of a TFSA.
