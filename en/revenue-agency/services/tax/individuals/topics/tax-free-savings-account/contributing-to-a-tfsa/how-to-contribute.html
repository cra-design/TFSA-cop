How to contribute to a TFSA

On this page

  • How to make a contribution
  • How to avoid an over-contribution

When to make a contribution

If you have available contribution room and are a resident of Canada, you may contribute funds to your TFSA at any time during the calendar year.

Make sure you have available room

Your contribution room can change when you make withdrawals and contributions. Always calculate your available contribution room before you contribute.

For more information:  Calculate your room before you contribute

If you become a non-resident, do not contribute to your TFSA.

For more information:

  • Do not contribute if you are a non-resident  
  • How non-residency affects your TFSA

How to make a contribution

To contribute funds to your TFSA, contact the financial institution that issued it. You can contribute to you TFSA at any time during the year, provided you have the available contribution room.

The amount that you contribute will immediately be applied against your available contribution room and reduce it by the same amount you contributed.

How to avoid an over-contribution

Do not contribute more than your available contribution room allows. If you over-contribute, the excess amount is taxable at a rate of 1% per month for as long as the excess amount remains in the account. For more information: If you over-contribute to a TFSA

Track your contribution room yourself

We recommend calculating your available contribution yourself using your own records. Neither your financial institution nor the CRA necessarily has your complete TFSA information.
For more information: Calculate your room before you contribute

Do not replace a withdrawal in the same year 

Never replace or repay a withdrawal in the same calendar year you made it, unless you know that you have enough unused contribution room available.

Contributions you make are immediately applied against your available contribution room, reducing your available room by the amount you contribute. But withdrawals from your TFSA do not create new available room until the next calendar year.

Example : Repaying contributions in the same year may lead to over-contribution

On January 1, 2025, Taylor’s available TFSA contribution room for the year is $6,500.

During the year, she makes the following transactions.

On January 10, 2025, she contributes $2,000. This contribution is immediately applied against her available room, reducing it to $4,500 (6,500-2,000).

On February 10, 2025, she withdraws $2,000. Her contribution room stays at $4,500. (The $2,000 room will be added back next year.)

On March 10, 2025, she contributes $2,000. Her contribution room immediately reduces to $2,500 (4,500-2,000).

On April 10, 2025, she withdraws $1,000. Her contribution room stays at $2, 500. (The $1,000 room will be added back next year.)

On Dec 31,2025, she contributes $6,500. Because she only has $2,500 room available, she has over-contributed by $4,000.

In 2025, her contributions added up as follows: $2000 + $2,000 + $6500 = $10,500.

In this example, she over-contributed and have exceeded her available contribution room for 2025 of $6,500 by $4,000.

Investment losses do not create contribution room

Changes in the value of your TFSA investments do not affect your TFSA contribution room.

If your TFSA contains an investment that loses value, the loss does not count as a withdrawal.  An investment loss is not added back as available contribution room the next year.

Example: Do not replace an investment loss

At the beginning of 2025, Sam’s available TFSA contribution room is $6,500.

During the year, he does the following transactions.  

  • On January 10, 2025, he contributes $6,500. He invests these funds in ABC stock. 
  • Over the year, ABC stock loses value.
  • On Dec 29, 2025, his investment is now worth $3,000.
  • On Dec 31, 2025, he contributes $3,000 to bring his balance back up to $6,500. 

Sam’s contributions for the year add up as follows: $6,500 + $3,000 = $9,500  
Because Same’s available room for the year was $6,500, he over-contributed by $3,000.

Do not over-contribute to multiple TFSAs

If you have multiple TFSAs, your available contribution room applies to all of them combined. To avoid over-contributing, be sure to track your transactions for all your accounts before making a contribution.
For more information: Calculate your room before you contribute

Example : Over-contributing to multiple accounts

Isla, an 18-year-old resident of Canada, opens her first TFSA in February 2022 with Rose Bank.

The TFSA annual dollar limit is $6,000 for 2022. Isla’s contribution room for 2022 is $6,000. She contributes $6,000 to this TFSA.

In September of the same year, Isla opens a second TFSA with Tropical Bank and contributes $6,000 to this TFSA.

Her contributions for that year add up as follows: $6,000 + $6,000 = $12,000.

Since her available contribution room for all her TFSAs combined was $6,000, Isla has over-contributed.

Isla is signed up for email notifications in her CRA account. In May 2023, she receives a warning letter from the CRA in her CRA account about the excess amount.  She reads the letter and removes the excess amount from her TFSA. Because of her quick action, Isla avoids paying tax on her overcontribution.

Now Isla keeps records of her contributions for all of her TFSAs and makes sure the total is within her available contribution room.

Do direct transfers through your TFSA issuer

If you withdraw your own funds and re-contribute them to another TFSA, you may unintentionally over-contribute to the TFSA. If you are transferring funds from one TFSA to another, always do a direct transfer through your financial institution to avoid tax implications.

For more information: Avoid doing your own transfers

Option to give to your spouse or common-law partner

The total contribution you make to your own TFSA cannot be more than your available contribution room.
However, you can give your spouse or common-law partner money so that they can contribute to their own TFSA. This amount or any income earned from this amount will not be allocated back to you.
The total contribution that your spouse or common-law partner makes cannot be more than their available contribution room.
For more information: Requesting a transfer
